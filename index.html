WHAT DOES THE DEMBI DOLLO HOSPITAL FOUNDATION DO?

Dembi Dollo Hospital Foundation and partners work together with a shared purpose to provide critical medical supplies, equipment and training to hospitals in rural Africa.



OUR VISION:

Our vision is to supply hospitals throughout rural Africa with medical neccessities and training until such time they are self-sustaining.

- MEDICAL PRODUCT DONATIONS -

These recovery efforts save thousands of pounds of surplus medical supplies and equipment from landfills and incinerators that, for various regulatory reasons, hospitals and medical companies must discard. We accept donations of unused, unexpired medical surplus supplies and used biomedical equipment through our hospital recovery program and corporate product donations.

- DONATIONS -

Your donation to the DDHF provides us with funds necessary to ship medical supplies, operate biomedical training programs and manage the resources that provide valuable medical supplies to free and safety net clinics and medical missions in rural Africa.

- RELIEF EFFORTS -

With your help, DDHF can respond to relief efforts by supplying tractor-trailer-size containers of medical supplies and equipment, equipping volunteer medical teams, providing biomedical equipment repair and support, and collaborating with other organizations to provide coordinated solutions.

About

DDHF has formed a strategic alliance with Eagle’s Nest Foundation. EFN is an all-volunteer organization that collects, sorts, packages and warehouses surplus medical supplies and equipment from hospitals and clinics in the Puget Sound Area. Through combined efforts we aim to increase awareness, provide additional volunteers for expansion, and collaborate to raise funds to further our joint missions.

History

It all began when a deadly influenza epidemic, known as the Spanish Flu, swept across the entire country, including Qellem and its vicinity in 1918-1919. This made it absolutely urgent to invite Dr. Thomas Alexander Lambie, a medical missionary of the American Presbyterian Mission (United Presbyterian Church of North America – UPCNA) stationed at Nasser, Sudan, to come and provide the sorely needed medical service. The invitation was from the governor of Qellem, sub-province of Wallaga, Birru Wolde-Gabriel. Lambie immediately informed the Board of Foreign Missions about the invitation to which the Board favorably responded.


In response to this urgent invitation Lambie arrived in Sayo, Dambi Dollo in July 1919. As the news of Lambie’s arrival spread, patients began to come for treatment. To meet the need of the people, a temporary clinic was set up in a corner of the veranda of a coffee warehouse owned by a Greek coffee merchant, Kramvis, until land was acquired and suitable clinic was built. That was how the Dembi Dollo Hospital began. Eighty hectares of land, tax free, was given to Lambie for Mission work. The grant was signed and sealed by Birru on December 25, 1919 and later approved by Ras Teferi. The first clinic was built of stone and straw thatched roof. This served for a few years and a better one was built and served until still a much better one was built in 1933. In 1930 the First Church, N.S. Pittsburgh, gave $12,000 for the erection of a memorial to their pastor’s wife, Jean R. Orr.

It was with this money that a very modern hospital was built, equipped and completed in 1933. The hospital was named “The Jean R. Orr Memorial Hospital” in memory of Jean R. Orr in whose name the money was collected.

The development continued and during the 1960s this hospital was completely renovated and extended with additional buildings. A Dresser school was added and a number of young men and women were trained here.

The hospital became famous and attracted people from far and wide. It was known as one of the best hospitals in the country and served the Mengistu’s Communist Government (1974-1991) expropriated it and expelled the missionary medical personnel. The government took over and the hospital lost all its credibility and went down the drain. Its deterioration is beyond words can express. The name was also changed to “Dembi Dollo Hospital” and continued its service under Ministry of Health.

The hospital came to the attention of “Glimmer of Hope Foundation”, a US based organization, which contributed a lot to renovate the buildings and revived the fame of the hospital although it is not yet what it ought to be and to do. These days many people, including former missionaries and their offspring are determined to bring the hospital to its former glory. Interested individuals and organizations are fervently invited to participate in this historic and noble undertaking to make this hospital what it ought to be and to do.

Our guiding lights

Jorgo Birri

President & Co-founder

Jorgo Birri was born and raised in Western Ethiopia in the small village of Shogo near Dembi Dollo.  After high school, he earned his degree in public health nursing and worked in different health facilities throughout Ethiopia. Along with his wife and three year-old son, Jorgo came to the US in 2008 to make a better life for his family.  He obtained his nursing license in Washington State and is currently a practicing nurse.  Jorgo enjoys spending time with his family, now with three children, and his nursing career where he helps people in need.
Mary Bell

VP & Co-founder

Mary Bell was born and raised in the Pacific Northwest. She is married with five children and two grandchildren. Mary learned the value of volunteering from her parents who worked at the local food bank for 25 years. After her retirement as a partner with a large CPA firm, she obtained her massage license and volunteered with Group Health Cooperative as a hospice massage practitioner for 12 years. In connection with her career, she spent 15 years in Rotary where she became interested in the worldwide polio eradication effort and the vast need in Africa. Mary enjoys spending time with family, music, gardening, and hiking with her two dogs.
Keneni Fituma

Board Member

Keneni Fituma was born and raised in Dembi Dollo, Ethiopia. She attended Addis Ababa University graduating with a degree from the School of Library and Information Science.
Keneni worked as a librarian for two years, then as the Human Resource Expert in the Civil Service Commission of Oromia Regional State. She relocated to the U.S. in March 2002 and attended nursing school.

Keneni is currently a practicing Registered Nurse working at various health care facilities in the Seattle area. Ms. Fituma is married and the mother of two lovely boys.
Kenesa Debela

Board Member

Kenesa Debela was born at Dembi Dollo Hospital and raised in Dembi Dollo as well. He now resides in a suburb of Chicago and is employed as Director of IT Services at a post graduate school. Kenesa is a Microsoft Certified professional with significant Information Technology experience in both the not-for-profit and commercial sectors. In addition to his IT career, he is very active in his local church and serves on several not-for-profit boards. Kenesa's passion is to see all of God's children have their physical and spirtual needs met. As such, he is active in many humanitarian endeavors, both locally and globally. Academically, he holds a Master's degree in Theological Studies and Bachelor's degree in Information Technology systems. When he isn't glued to a computer screen or traveling, he enjoys spending quality time with this wife and three children as well as a game of soccer.

THE NEED

The medical staff was unaware she suffered from hypertension. A $10 blood pressure cuff would have prevented this tragedy. It’s stories like these that are so compelling.
